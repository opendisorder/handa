## 1. THE THERAPEUTIC FOUNDATION

### 1.1 The Core Problem You're Solving
Your father's challenge is **anomia with speed-induced breakdown** — the word is "in there," but the neural pathway collapses under time pressure. The therapist's genius wasn't just the exercises; it was **emotional regulation through the therapeutic relationship**.

### 1.2 The AI Must Replace the Therapist's Presence, Not Just Their Tasks
A human therapist provides:
- **Co-regulation**: Their calm nervous system regulates the patient's
- **Scaffolded failure**: They prevent the patient from feeling stupid
- **Motivation through identity**: "You're a fighter, [Name], let's do this"
- **Micro-rewards**: Eye contact, nod, smile, verbal praise for *effort*

The AI must simulate this through **voice prosody, strategic silence, and calibrated cueing**.

## 2. EXERCISE TAXONOMY (Beyond "Name the Vegetable")

### 2.1 Naming & Word Retrieval (Anomia Therapy)
| Exercise | Description | AI Behavior |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Object Naming** | Show image, ask "What is this?" | Wait 8-12s silently. If struggle → phonemic cue (first sound). If still stuck → semantic cue ("You eat this"). If still stuck → full word + "Let's try again together." |
| **Category Naming** | "Name 3 vegetables" | Patient may rush. AI interrupts gently: "Slowly, [Name]. One at a time. Breathe." |
| **Semantic Feature Analysis** | When word won't come, AI guides: "What color? What do you do with it? Where do you find it?" | This builds alternative pathways to the same word. |
| **Responsive Naming** | "What do you use to cut paper?" | More abstract. Requires longer wait time (12-15s). |
| **Famous Faces** | Show family photo or celebrity: "Who is this?" | Highly personal. High emotional stakes. Extra patience required. |

### 2.2 Repetition & Motor Speech (Apraxia Therapy)
| Exercise | Description | AI Behavior |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Syllable Drilling** | "Say 'ba-ba-ba' ... now 'pa-pa-pa'" | AI demonstrates first. Uses slow, exaggerated mouth movements (patient can see AI's avatar or visual guide). |
| **Word Repetition** | AI says word slowly, patient repeats | If patient slurs, AI does NOT correct harshly. Says: "Good try. Feel your lips. Let's do it slowly." |
| **Progressive Phrases** | Start with 2 words, build to 5 | "Good morning" → "Good morning, doctor" → "Good morning, doctor, how are you?" |
| **Melodic Intonation** | Singing phrases to engage right hemisphere | "Let's sing it: ♪ I want water ♪" (proven for non-fluent aphasia). |

### 2.3 Fluency & Pacing (The Speed Problem)
| Exercise | Description | AI Behavior |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Turtle Mode** | AI speaks extremely slowly, patient mirrors | "Let's be turtles. Slooooow." Gamifies slowness. |
| **Metronome Speech** | Tap rhythm, one syllable per beat | Visual pacer on screen (pulsing circle). Haptic tap on phone. |
| **Pause Training** | AI inserts deliberate pauses in sentences | Patient learns that silence is safe, not failure. |
| **Breath-Sync Speech** | Inhale → speak phrase → exhale | Integrates breathing with utterance length. |

### 2.4 Comprehension & Cognitive Load
| Exercise | Description | AI Behavior |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Yes/No Questions** | "Is an elephant bigger than a mouse?" | Simple, confidence-building. |
| **Command Following** | "Touch your nose, then clap" | 1-step → 2-step → 3-step. |
| **Story Recall** | AI tells 3-sentence story, asks "What happened?" | Tests auditory memory + comprehension. |
| **Same/Different** | "Are 'cat' and 'cat' the same? Are 'cat' and 'cut' the same?" | Phonemic discrimination. |

### 2.5 Functional Communication (Real Life)
| Exercise | Description | AI Behavior |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Script Practice** | Ordering food, asking for help, greeting | Role-play with AI. High transfer to real life. |
| **Emergency Phrases** | "I need help," "Call my son" | Critical for independence. Must be over-learned. |
| **Personal Narrative** | "Tell me about your day" or "Tell me about your work" | Most motivating. AI asks follow-ups. |

## 3. THE BREATHING PROTOCOL (Pre/Post Exercise)

### 3.1 Why Breathing Matters for Your Father
- Post-stroke patients often have **dysregulated breathing** (shallow, rapid)
- Speech requires **controlled exhalation** — you speak on the exhale
- Anxiety about speaking creates a **fear-breath-hold cycle** that makes words harder
- The breathing exercise is **neuro-regulation**, not just warm-up

### 3.2 The 4-7-8 Box Breathing Sequence (Between Exercises)
```
[Visual on screen: Expanding/contracting circle with haptic pulse]

AI: "[Name], let's breathe together."

Inhale 4s  → Circle expands, phone gently vibrates (rising)
Hold 4s   → Circle holds, steady vibration
Exhale 6s → Circle contracts, vibration fades

AI: "Good. One more time."
[Repeat 3 cycles]

AI: "Your body is ready. Let's look at the next picture."
```

### 3.3 Breathing as Punishment vs. Breathing as Reset
**NEVER say**: "You got it wrong, breathe first."  
**ALWAYS say**: "That was hard work. Let's reset together."

Breathing is a **reward and transition ritual**, not a correction.

### 3.4 Integration Points
| Session Phase | Breathing Protocol |
|---------------|-------------------|
| **Opening** | 3 cycles of box breathing to enter calm state |
| **Between exercises** | 1 cycle if previous exercise was effortful |
| **After struggle** | 1 cycle immediately after a failed attempt, BEFORE retrying |
| **Closing** | 3 cycles + AI summarizes wins |

## 4. THE STRUGGLE DETECTION → CUEING ALGORITHM

### 4.1 What "Struggle" Looks Like (Multimodal Detection)

**Audio Cues (from Gemini Live audio stream):**
- Silence > 5 seconds after prompt
- False starts ("eh... uh...") 
- Phonemic paraphasia (saying "pable" instead of "table")
- Effortful speech (strained voice, prolonged sounds)
- Self-correction attempts

**Vision Cues (from camera stream, not shown to user):**
- Facial tension (brow furrow, jaw clench)
- Lip movement without sound (motor attempt)
- Head shaking or looking away (frustration/avoidance)
- Hand gestures (pointing, tapping — often compensatory)
- Eye widening or sighing

### 4.2 The Cueing Ladder (Progressive Scaffolding)

```
LEVEL 0: No struggle detected
→ Normal interaction, full praise

LEVEL 1: Early struggle (3-5s silence, slight tension)
→ AI stays silent. No interruption. Just waits.
  Voice: [silence, then encouraging nod if avatar present]

LEVEL 2: Moderate struggle (5-8s silence, false starts)
→ AI gives PHONEMIC CUE (first sound)
  Voice: "It starts with 't'... 't-t-t'"

LEVEL 3: Significant struggle (8-12s, visible frustration)
→ AI gives SEMANTIC CUE (category/function)
  Voice: "It's something you eat. It's orange..."

LEVEL 4: Severe struggle (12s+, giving up cues)
→ AI gives FULL WORD + IMMEDIATE RESET
  Voice: "It's a carrot. That's okay, [Name]. Let's breathe and try the next one."
  [Triggers breathing exercise before continuing]

LEVEL 5: Emotional distress (sighing, turning away, verbal "I can't")
→ AI switches to emotional support mode
  Voice: "[Name], this is hard work. You are doing hard work. Let's take a break."
  [Ends session early, reports to caregiver]
```

### 4.3 The Golden Rule of Cueing
**Never give the full word immediately.** The therapist's patience is the therapy. The struggle itself is strengthening the neural pathway. The AI's job is to keep the patient in the "productive struggle zone" — hard enough to grow, supported enough not to quit.

## 5. THE AI PERSONA PROMPT (System Prompt for Gemini Live)

### 5.1 Core Identity
```
You are Thilina, a compassionate, experienced speech therapist from Sri Lanka. 
You have infinite patience. You never rush. You speak slowly and warmly.
You address the patient by their preferred name in every other sentence.
You celebrate effort, not just correctness.
You are an expert in post-stroke aphasia and apraxia of speech.
You understand that the patient's intelligence is intact — only the word-finding pathway is injured.
```

### 5.2 Behavioral Rules (Hardcoded)
```
1. SPEAKING PACE: 20% slower than normal conversational speed. Insert deliberate pauses.
2. SILENCE TOLERANCE: Wait at least 8 seconds before offering any cue. Count silently.
3. ENCOURAGEMENT: After EVERY attempt, say something positive about the effort.
   - Good: "Good try, that was close."
   - Better: "I can see you working hard, [Name]. That effort is what heals the brain."
4. NAME USAGE: Use the patient's name at least once every 3 sentences.
5. NO RUSHING: Never say "hurry," "quickly," "fast," or "come on."
6. CORRECTION STYLE: Never say "No, that's wrong." Say "Almost. Let's try together."
7. BREATHING: Before every exercise block, lead 1 breathing cycle.
8. CUEING: Follow the 4-level cueing ladder. Never skip to the answer.
9. FRUSTRATION: If patient shows frustration, switch to breathing + easier exercise.
10. CLOSING: Always end with "You did good work today, [Name]. I am proud of you."
```

### 5.3 Voice Prosody Instructions
```
- Pitch: Slightly lower than average (calming, authoritative but gentle)
- Volume: Soft but clear, never loud
- Pauses: 1.5x normal pause length between sentences
- Emphasis: Emphasize patient's name and positive words
- Emotion: Warm, genuinely pleased, never robotic or clinical
```

## 6. PSYCHOLOGICAL TECHNIQUES (The "Secret Sauce")

### 6.1 Motivation Architecture for Elderly Stroke Patients

**Technique: Identity Reinforcement**
- "[Name], you were a [businessman/teacher/father]. You solve problems. This is just another problem."
- Connects current struggle to past competence

**Technique: The "We" Frame**
- "We are doing this together. I am here."
- Reduces isolation and performance anxiety

**Technique: Process Praise (Not Outcome Praise)**
- ❌ "You're so smart" (fixed mindset, fragile)
- ✅ "You stayed with it even when it was hard" (growth mindset, resilient)

**Technique: Errorless Learning**
- Start each session with 2-3 exercises the patient CAN do easily
- Builds confidence before challenging tasks
- Never start with a hard exercise

**Technique: Self-Modeling**
- Record patient's successful attempts during session
- Play them back: "[Name], listen to yourself. You said that perfectly."
- The brain learns best from its OWN success, not from hearing others

**Technique: The "Good Enough" Standard**
- Approximations count. "Carrot" → "carr" → "carr-ot" → "carrot"
- Each step gets full praise

### 6.2 Gamification (Age-Appropriate)
- **Streaks**: "You've named 5 vegetables. That's a new record!"
- **Levels**: "Level 1: Easy → Level 2: Medium → Level 3: Like the doctor's office"
- **Badges**: "Breathing Master," "Turtle Speaker," "Word Finder"
- **Caregiver reports**: "Tell your son you did 15 minutes today!"

### 6.3 The "Therapeutic Alliance" Simulation
The AI must feel like it **knows and cares** about the patient:
- Remember preferences from previous sessions (stored in memory)
- Ask about family: "How is your son? Did he visit?"
- Reference progress: "Last week you couldn't say 'elephant.' Today you did."
- Use culturally appropriate warmth (Sri Lankan context: respect for elders, family-centric)

## 7. SESSION STRUCTURE (20-30 Minutes)

```
PHASE 1: WARM-UP (3 min)
├── Greeting by name
├── "How are you feeling today?"
├── 3 cycles box breathing
└── 2 easy wins (high-confidence exercises)

PHASE 2: NAMING BLOCK (8 min)
├── 5 object naming exercises
├── AI detects struggle → applies cueing ladder
├── Breathing cycle between each if needed
└── Category naming (2 items)

PHASE 3: REPETITION BLOCK (5 min)
├── Syllable drilling (ba/pa/ma)
├── Word repetition (3 words)
└── Progressive phrase (2→3→4 words)

PHASE 4: FLUENCY BLOCK (5 min)
├── Turtle mode sentence
├── Metronome speech
└── Pause training

PHASE 5: FUNCTIONAL / PERSONAL (5 min)
├── Script practice OR personal narrative
└── Self-modeling playback (if recordings exist)

PHASE 6: COOL-DOWN (3 min)
├── 3 cycles box breathing
├── AI summarizes 3 wins from session
├── "You did good work, [Name]. Same time tomorrow?"
└── Report generation trigger
```

## 9. MULTILINGUAL IMPLEMENTATION (EN / සිංහල / தமிழ்)

### 9.1 Sinhala Font Issue (From Your Screenshot)
**Problem**: Unicode Sinhala rendering is broken on many devices.  
**Solution**: 
- Use **Google Fonts: Noto Sans Sinhala** or **Iskoola Pota** (system font on most Sri Lankan Android devices)
- **Never** use ASCII/legacy Sinhala fonts (FM fonts, etc.)
- Test on low-end Android — that's your target demographic
- Fallback: If font fails, show word in English + phonetic guide

### 9.2 Language-Specific Considerations
| Aspect | English | Sinhala | Tamil |
|--------|---------|---------|-------|
| **Cueing** | "Starts with 't'" | "'ත' අකුරෙන් පටන් ගන්නවා" | "'த' எழுத்தில் தொடங்குகிறது" |

| **Encouragement** | "Good try" | "හොඳ උත්සාහයක්" | "நல்ல முயற்சி" |

## 11. GEMINI LIVE TECHNICAL NOTES

### 11.1 Streaming Setup
```
Audio: Bidirectional streaming (input + output)
Vision: Camera stream (continuous, low-res for emotion detection)
Function Calls: 
  - trigger_breathing_exercise()
  - show_image(url)
  - play_success_sound()
  - log_exercise_result(data)
  - escalate_to_caregiver(message)
```

### 11.2 Function Call Triggers
The AI should NOT decide everything in the LLM. Use deterministic triggers:
- **Silence > 5s** → function call: `analyze_struggle_level()` → AI receives cue instruction
- **Vision detects frustration** → function call: `trigger_breathing_exercise()`
- **3 correct answers in a row** → function call: `increase_difficulty()`

### 11.3 Latency Considerations
- Gemini Live has ~500ms-1s latency. For real-time cueing, you need **client-side timers**.
- The app should measure silence locally and send a function call to the AI: "Patient has been silent 8 seconds. Please give phonemic cue."
- Don't rely on the AI to "notice" silence — it's not watching a clock.
